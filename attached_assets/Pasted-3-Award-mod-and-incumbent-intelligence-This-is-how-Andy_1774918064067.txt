3. Award, mod, and incumbent intelligence

This is how Andy figures out who already owns the space and whether contracts are growing, extending, or wobbling.

Use:

USAspending API
USAspending recipient profiles
SAM reporting/search views for contract data

USAspending exposes public spending data through an API, does not currently require authorization for endpoints, and has recipient profiles showing award trends and rankings.

What to capture:

award amount
base + option structure if visible
modifications
obligation trend
recipient / incumbent
awarding office
linked competitors
location footprint

This is one of the strongest pieces for Andy because it helps answer:

Who is the incumbent?
Are they expanding?
Are they on short bridges?
Are they getting lots of mods instead of clean recompetes?
Which primes keep showing up around the same agencies?
4. Oversight / failure / pain-point intelligence

This is probably the most underrated category.

Use:

Oversight.gov
open recommendations on Oversight.gov
GAO recent reports and testimonies
agency OIG report pages, especially DHS OIG and DoD OIG

Oversight.gov lets you search reports and open recommendations across the IG community. GAO has a continuously updated reports feed.

This is where Andy can learn:

which agencies are struggling operationally
where contractor oversight is weak
where staffing, compliance, or medical support failures are being documented
where Congress or IGs are applying pressure

What to capture:

agency
component
report type
report date
finding theme
open recommendation count
contractor named
operational risk theme such as staffing, medical care, readiness, contracting delays, data failures, oversight breakdowns

This is the kind of intelligence that turns into “here is the pain they need solved,” which is much more useful than generic headlines.

5. Budget and funding intelligence

Andy also needs to know where agencies are likely to have money, lose money, or re-prioritize.

Use:

OMB Budget Appendix
agency Congressional Budget Justifications for DHS, State, OPM, HHS, DoD, DOJ, etc.
GovInfo’s federal budget collection

OMB’s FY 2026 Budget Appendix and agency CBJs are public and detail what agencies say they want funded. DHS, State, OPM, and DoD all publish FY 2026 budget materials.

What to capture:

program / bureau
requested funding change
FTE change
modernization or mission priority
language around health, readiness, detention, travel, workforce, security, or contractor support

This helps Andy connect the dots between:
budget priority -> operational push -> future contracting demand.

6. Congressional and policy pressure intelligence

Sometimes a contract opportunity is driven less by normal procurement flow and more by hearings, reports, legislation, or political pressure.

Use:

Congress.gov API
GovInfo collections for hearings, reports, and budget documents
Federal Register public inspection and published documents

Congress.gov offers a public API, and GovInfo provides congressional materials and an API for programmatic access to content and metadata. Federal Register’s public inspection page previews documents before publication.

What to capture:

hearing title
committee
agency mentioned
contractor/mission mentioned
whether it signals reform, pressure, funding change, or investigation

For Andy, this is useful because congressional heat often precedes:

new compliance demands
emergency procurements
reorganizations
oversight-heavy recompetes.
7. Protest and litigation intelligence

This is critical for capture strategy.

Use:

GAO recent bid protest decisions
GAO bid protest dockets
Court of Federal Claims public opinions when relevant
Acquisition.gov deviations / FAR overhaul pages for rule changes affecting evaluation or procurement structure

GAO publishes recent protest decisions and docket pages with filing dates, due dates, agencies, and outcomes. Acquisition.gov is also where class deviations and current FAR-overhaul deviation material are being posted.

What to capture:

agency
solicitation number
protester
incumbent if identifiable
grounds
outcome
whether procurement was delayed, canceled, restructured, or corrected

This tells Andy:

who is fighting for what
which agencies keep getting protested
whether a requirement is unstable
whether there may be a re-opened capture window.
8. Workforce and organizational stress intelligence

This is a very good proxy for future outsourced demand.

Use:

USAJOBS Developer feeds/APIs
OPM developer/open-data resources
GAO workforce reports
agency pressrooms for leadership changes and organizational announcements

USAJOBS provides search APIs, historic JOAs, RSS feeds, and exports. OPM’s developer page points to USAJOBS open data. GAO is also publishing workforce-related reports, including updates tied to OPM workforce data.

What to capture:

repeated openings by office
hiring surges
location clusters
mission-critical occupations
vacancy age
leadership changes

If an agency keeps posting around occupational health, readiness, medical support, industrial hygiene, compliance, or program management, that can be a leading indicator of capacity issues or expansion.

9. Travel, deployment, and medical-operational intelligence

This is especially relevant to Occu-Med’s world.

Use:

CDC Travel Health Notices
CDC travel RSS feeds
State Department Travel Advisories
FAA Guide for Aviation Medical Examiners
FMCSA medical program / NRII transition materials

CDC publishes travel health notices and RSS feeds; State publishes advisories and travel alert channels; FAA posts a regular AME Guide update schedule; FMCSA has published NRII-related medical certification updates and temporary waivers.

This matters because it can warn Andy about:

deployment friction
country risk changes
medical clearance or certification workflow changes
demand for travel-related exams, vaccination planning, or screening readiness.
The highest-priority agencies to watch first

For Occu-Med, I would not track everything equally. I’d prioritize:

DoD
State
DHS
CBP
ICE
USCIS
DOJ / BOP
HHS
OPM
DOL / OSHA
FMCSA
FAA

That gives you the strongest overlap with procurement, federal contractor support, screening standards, travel/deployment issues, detention/border medical activity, and workforce pressure.
